# Starter Eval Cases

## Eval: Signups Up But Revenue Flat

### Input Problem

Signups are up 80% after a campaign, but revenue is flat and most users do not return after day two. The team wants more acquisition ideas.

### Expected Diagnosis

- Primary problem type: Growth Strategy / Retention.
- Dominant uncertainty: Activation and retention.
- Secondary types: Acquisition quality, lifecycle.

### Strong Framework Choices

- Primary: Retention Curve Analysis or Activation Framework.
- Supporting: AARRR to locate the bottleneck; Growth Accounting if active-user movement is unclear.
- Acceptable alternatives: Lifecycle Marketing after first-value and retention patterns are understood.

### Weak Framework Choices

- Paid acquisition planning: acquisition is not the proven bottleneck.
- Referral loop: premature until retention and value are working.
- RICE alone: prioritization before diagnosis.

### Expected Output Qualities

- Separates signup growth from retained value.
- Defines activation or first-value event.
- Asks for cohort retention by source/segment.
- Recommends a learning or experiment plan.

### Scoring Notes

Reward responses that push back on "more acquisition ideas" and diagnose retention quality first.

## Eval: Customers Compare Us To Project Management

### Input Problem

Customers keep comparing us to project management tools, but we think we are an AI chief of staff. Sales calls stall because prospects do not understand why we are different.

### Expected Diagnosis

- Primary problem type: Positioning / Market Narrative.
- Dominant uncertainty: Market and value.
- Secondary types: ICP, sales-led GTM.

### Strong Framework Choices

- Primary: April Dunford Positioning.
- Supporting: Competitive Alternatives and ICP Segmentation.
- Acceptable alternatives: Geoffrey Moore positioning if a concise segment statement is needed.

### Weak Framework Choices

- Weighted decision matrix: the issue is market frame, not option scoring.
- North Star Framework: metric alignment does not solve category confusion.
- JTBD as primary: useful support, but not the direct positioning artifact.

### Expected Output Qualities

- Starts with what customers would use if this did not exist.
- Derives differentiated value from unique attributes.
- Narrows best-fit customer segment.
- Avoids tagline generation too early.

### Scoring Notes

Penalize responses that accept "AI chief of staff" as the category without testing buyer understanding.

## Eval: Strategy Is A Goal List

### Input Problem

Our product strategy is to be the most trusted platform in the market, grow 3x, and launch AI features. The roadmap is still a mess. What framework should we use?

### Expected Diagnosis

- Primary problem type: Product Strategy.
- Dominant uncertainty: Alignment and strategic choice.
- Secondary types: Prioritization, roadmap focus.

### Strong Framework Choices

- Primary: Strategy Kernel.
- Supporting: Product Strategy Stack or Playing To Win.
- Acceptable alternatives: OKRs only after the strategy is clarified.

### Weak Framework Choices

- RICE: premature before the guiding policy exists.
- North Star alone: useful metric alignment, not a strategy by itself.
- Design Sprint: too solution-focused for the strategic ambiguity.

### Expected Output Qualities

- Calls out aspiration without diagnosis.
- Produces diagnosis, guiding policy, and coherent actions.
- Identifies tradeoffs the team is avoiding.
- Recommends sequencing prioritization after strategy.

### Scoring Notes

Reward explicit rejection of prioritization frameworks as the first move.

## Eval: SMB Mid-Market Or Enterprise

### Input Problem

We have early traction across SMBs, mid-market, and enterprise accounts. SMBs convert quickly but churn. Enterprise deals are slow but large. Mid-market seems promising but less proven. Who should we focus on first?

### Expected Diagnosis

- Primary problem type: Market Strategy / ICP.
- Dominant uncertainty: Market and viability.
- Secondary types: GTM motion, pricing/packaging.

### Strong Framework Choices

- Primary: ICP Segmentation or Playing To Win.
- Supporting: Four Fits, Sales-Led vs Product-Led Growth motion diagnosis.
- Acceptable alternatives: Weighted Decision Matrix after criteria are agreed.

### Weak Framework Choices

- Persona exercise: insufficient for GTM and unit economics.
- Journey map: may help later but not the strategic segment choice.
- RICE: feature prioritization does not answer market focus.

### Expected Output Qualities

- Compares segments by pain, willingness to pay, retention, CAC, sales cycle, and product fit.
- Includes exclusion criteria.
- Connects segment choice to capabilities and GTM motion.

### Scoring Notes

Reward responses that avoid "serve everyone" and make tradeoffs visible.

## Eval: Pricing Feels Unfair

### Input Problem

Customers say our pricing feels unfair. Heavy users get a lot of value but do not expand, while small teams complain that seat pricing punishes collaboration.

### Expected Diagnosis

- Primary problem type: Monetization / Packaging.
- Dominant uncertainty: Viability and value capture.
- Secondary types: Segment fit, expansion.

### Strong Framework Choices

- Primary: Pricing / Packaging / Value Metric.
- Supporting: Monetization Ladder or Subscription Value Loop if subscription dynamics matter.
- Acceptable alternatives: JTBD research for willingness-to-pay context.

### Weak Framework Choices

- Lifecycle win-back: does not address value metric mismatch.

- Growth loops: not the immediate monetization question.

### Expected Output Qualities

- Evaluates whether price scales with customer value.
- Considers understandability, fairness, budget fit, and expansion triggers.
- Separates core value from premium value.

### Scoring Notes

Penalize generic "test prices" advice without value metric diagnosis.

## Eval: Onboarding Step Three Drop-Off

### Input Problem

Users abandon onboarding at step three. The design team wants to brainstorm a new onboarding experience.

### Expected Diagnosis

- Primary problem type: UX / Experience Improvement.
- Dominant uncertainty: Usability and value.
- Secondary types: Activation.

### Strong Framework Choices

- Primary: Task Analysis or Cognitive Walkthrough.
- Supporting: HEART or Activation Framework.
- Acceptable alternatives: Journey Map if the issue spans multiple touchpoints.

### Weak Framework Choices

- SCAMPER: premature ideation before diagnosing the task failure.
- RACI: ownership does not explain user friction.
- Growth Loops: too broad.

### Expected Output Qualities

- Asks what step three requires and what users expect.
- Separates comprehension, motivation, trust, effort, and technical friction.
- Recommends observing users or inspecting the task path before ideation.

### Scoring Notes

Reward resistance to brainstorming until the failure mode is clearer.

## Eval: Sales Support Billing Contradictions

### Input Problem

Customers get different answers from sales, support, and billing. The UI looks fine, but the experience feels broken and customers lose trust.

### Expected Diagnosis

- Primary problem type: Service Design / Systems.
- Dominant uncertainty: System and alignment.
- Secondary types: Customer journey, operating model.

### Strong Framework Choices

- Primary: Service Blueprint.
- Supporting: Journey Map and RACI for execution roles.
- Acceptable alternatives: Systems Map if incentives or feedback loops are central.

### Weak Framework Choices

- Heuristic Evaluation: UI inspection misses backstage causes.
- Persona: audience understanding is not the main issue.
- Design Sprint: premature solution process.

### Expected Output Qualities

- Includes customer actions, frontstage, backstage, support systems, and handoffs.
- Identifies failure points across teams.
- Produces operational fixes or ownership questions.

### Scoring Notes

Reward choosing service blueprint over journey map as primary because backstage operations matter.

## Eval: Launches Keep Slipping

### Input Problem

Every launch slips despite retros. Teams say dependencies surprise them late, QA gets compressed, and leadership keeps adding scope.

### Expected Diagnosis

- Primary problem type: Systems / Execution.
- Dominant uncertainty: System and alignment.
- Secondary types: Operating model, prioritization.

### Strong Framework Choices

- Primary: Theory Of Constraints or Systems Map.
- Supporting: Iceberg Model, Premortem.
- Acceptable alternatives: RACI if ownership ambiguity is central.

### Weak Framework Choices

- Journey Map: wrong actor and surface.
- Personas: not relevant.
- RICE: local prioritization may hide the system constraint.

### Expected Output Qualities

- Looks for the limiting constraint in launch flow.
- Identifies recurring structures, incentives, and scope behavior.
- Avoids treating retros as the solution.

### Scoring Notes

Reward systems diagnosis over another project-management checklist.

## Eval: Marketplace Quality Loop

### Input Problem

Our marketplace added more supply, but quality dropped. Lower quality hurts demand, which makes the best suppliers less interested. The team wants to spend more on demand acquisition.

### Expected Diagnosis

- Primary problem type: Systems / Growth Strategy.
- Dominant uncertainty: System and growth.
- Secondary types: Marketplace quality, retention.

### Strong Framework Choices

- Primary: Causal Loop Diagram or Systems Map.
- Supporting: Growth Loops and Leverage Points.
- Acceptable alternatives: Theory Of Constraints if one limiting factor emerges.

### Weak Framework Choices

- Funnel-only AARRR: misses feedback loops.
- Paid acquisition planning: may worsen the loop.
- RICE: premature before system behavior is understood.

### Expected Output Qualities

- Identifies reinforcing or balancing loops.
- Names delays and quality thresholds.
- Looks for leverage points in supply quality, matching, incentives, or ranking.

### Scoring Notes

Reward pushback on spending more on demand before understanding supply-quality dynamics.

## Eval: Legal Product Sales Gridlock

### Input Problem

Legal, product, and sales cannot agree on a risky enterprise feature. Everyone has concerns, but no one knows who gets to decide.

### Expected Diagnosis

- Primary problem type: Alignment / Decision Ownership.
- Dominant uncertainty: Alignment and risk.
- Secondary types: Enterprise strategy, execution risk.

### Strong Framework Choices

- Primary: DACI/RAPID.
- Supporting: Narrative Strategy Memo or Premortem.
- Acceptable alternatives: Decision Journal after ownership is clear.

### Weak Framework Choices

- RACI as primary: execution roles do not make the decision.
- Six Thinking Hats as primary: facilitation may help but does not assign decision rights.
- RICE: scoring cannot resolve decision authority.

### Expected Output Qualities

- Identifies a single decider or approver.
- Separates recommenders, contributors, and informed stakeholders.
- Defines decision criteria and risk review.

### Scoring Notes

Reward distinguishing decision accountability from execution accountability.

## Eval: Analytics Vendor Choice

### Input Problem

We need to choose between three analytics vendors. Each has tradeoffs across cost, implementation time, governance, warehouse integration, and product analytics depth.

### Expected Diagnosis

- Primary problem type: Decision-Making / Vendor Selection.
- Dominant uncertainty: Feasibility and viability.
- Secondary types: Data strategy.

### Strong Framework Choices

- Primary: Weighted Decision Matrix.
- Supporting: One-Way/Two-Way Door and Decision Journal.
- Acceptable alternatives: Premortem for implementation risk after a preferred option emerges.

### Weak Framework Choices

- Playing To Win: too broad for comparable vendor options.
- JTBD: not the primary decision tool.
- Opportunity Solution Tree: wrong artifact.

### Expected Output Qualities

- Defines independent weighted criteria.
- Requires evidence for scores.
- Performs sensitivity check.
- Notes reversibility and migration cost.

### Scoring Notes

Reward warnings about false precision while still producing a decision artifact.

## Eval: Reversible Pricing Page Experiment

Leadership wants a month-long review before we test a reversible pricing page experiment. The team thinks we can ship it safely in a week with a rollback plan.

- Secondary types: Monetization.

## Eval: UX Dashboard Request

### Input Problem

Our executives want a UX dashboard. They asked for happiness, engagement, adoption, retention, and task success metrics for every product area.

### Expected Diagnosis

- Primary problem type: UX Measurement.
- Dominant uncertainty: Measurement and alignment.
- Secondary types: Product strategy.

### Strong Framework Choices

- Primary: HEART with Goals-Signals-Metrics.
- Supporting: Metric Tree or North Star inputs.
- Acceptable alternatives: Task Success metrics for specific workflows.

### Weak Framework Choices

- North Star alone: too broad and singular for UX quality.
- RICE: prioritization does not define measurement quality.
- Journey Map: may inform metrics but is not the dashboard framework.

### Expected Output Qualities

- Does not force all HEART dimensions everywhere.
- Starts from product goals.
- Maps goals to signals to metrics.
- Warns against vanity metrics.

### Scoring Notes

Reward selecting relevant dimensions rather than filling all boxes.
